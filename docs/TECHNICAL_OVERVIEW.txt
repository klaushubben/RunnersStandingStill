RUNNERS STANDING STILL — TECHNICAL OVERVIEW
============================================

Source: current/source/sketch.runners-standing-still.js

The piece was conceived as a love letter to classic Perlin noise, read
through the lens of a sound looping device. A deterministic "recorded
phrase" is rendered once from the token hash; after that, a live loop
passes over the same material forever, one row per frame, while fetched
Ethereum block data quietly re-tunes the machine every few blocks. The
sections below walk through the three systems that carry that idea:
the layered noise, the procedural palette, and the block-data plumbing.


1. LAYERED USE OF PERLIN NOISE
------------------------------

The noise engine is a from-scratch implementation of Ken Perlin's
improved noise, not a library call. The NoiseField class builds a
256-entry permutation table shuffled by a seeded linear congruential
generator (so the field itself is reproducible from the token hash),
and implements 3D noise (perlin3) with the classic quintic fade curve
6t^5 - 15t^4 + 10t^3 and hashed gradient directions. This is
deliberately the textbook algorithm — the love letter is written in
the original handwriting.

On top of that raw field, the noise is layered three ways:

a) Fractal layering (fbm3). Octaves are summed at doubling
   frequencies with a per-octave amplitude falloff, then normalized by
   total amplitude. The piece runs 2 octaves with a very steep falloff
   (0.05), so the second octave is barely audible — a fundamental with
   a faint overtone rather than a full harmonic stack.

b) Dual-voice blending. Every sample point is actually two independent
   reads of the field: a "base" voice at the working frequency, and a
   "harmonic" voice at 1x–3.5x that frequency (harmonicScale), offset
   in the z dimension by +9.73 so the two voices are decorrelated. The
   two are combined with a simple linear mix whose blend knob is
   harmonicAmount (usually 0.16–0.4, occasionally near zero). In looper
   terms: an oscillator and its overtone on a mix fader.

c) Statistical centering (measureNoiseStats). Before drawing, the
   blended field is sampled on a 32px grid to measure its actual mean,
   and every subsequent sample is re-centered around 0.5 by subtracting
   that mean. This is DC-offset removal, exactly as you would do to an
   audio signal before amplifying it: without it, hash-specific bias in
   the field would make the whole image drift up or down the canvas.

The noise is not used to color pixels directly. Instead it drives a
melt integrator: for each column, the centered noise value (minus a
per-token noiseBias) is multiplied by meltStrength to give the height h
of a thin rectangle sampled from the source texture, and h is then
accumulated into a running per-column offset (deterministicColumnMelt /
liveColumnMelt). The image is therefore the integral of the noise field
over y — a random walk per column — which is why the texture appears to
melt and smear downward. noiseBias, targetDrift, verticalStretch and
tilt shape the overall slope of that walk, so the composition leans and
drifts in a controlled way while the fine structure stays pure Perlin.


2. PROCEDURAL COLOR PALETTE CONSTRUCTION
----------------------------------------

All color math happens in OKLab / OKLCH, with sRGB only as the final
output encoding. The pipeline includes hand-rolled conversions in both
directions, and two safety mechanisms:

- Gamut mapping by chroma reduction: oklchToHex repeatedly multiplies
  chroma by 0.94 until the resulting RGB lands inside [0,1], so
  requested colors desaturate gracefully instead of clipping.

- Perceptual hue damping: hueDamping attenuates chroma near green
  (135 deg) and purple (292 deg), the two hue regions where saturated
  OKLCH colors most easily turn garish on screen.

The palette parameters are not drawn from the PRNG stream — they are
sliced directly from bit ranges of the token hash (deriveHashTraits),
mirroring an on-chain renderer so that SVG thumbnail, metadata, and
canvas agree for any hash. The base hue comes from a weighted band
table (some hue neighborhoods are far more likely than others), and
further slices pick the palette family, spread, accent offset, split
angle, and background behavior.

Two palette families are built procedurally from those numbers:

- "div" (diverging): five stops that fan out from the base hue by
  +/- half of paletteDivergeSpread, with a lightness arc that rises
  from dark (L=0.36) through a pale center (L=0.82) and back down —
  a symmetric ridge, dark-saturated at the ends and airy in the middle.

- "split": a four-step monochromatic ramp on a "main" hue (light to
  dark, with small hue rotation per step so the ramp isn't sterile),
  plus one accent hue derived from a color-harmony relationship:
  near-complement, wide split, warm analog, or split-tertiary
  (relationshipAccentHue). A separate splitRole trait decides where the
  accent sits in the five-color sequence — pale highlight, mid-frame
  voice, deep anchor, or two accents bracketing the ramp.

A global constant, PALETTE_CHROMA_PUSH = 1.32, uniformly overdrives
chroma before gamut mapping pulls it back — asking for more color than
the screen can give and letting the mapper find the loudest legal value.

Everything downstream is derived rather than picked: dark and light
background colors are re-synthesized in OKLCH from the palette's own
swatches (paletteDarkBackground / paletteLightBackground), the "black
and white" stripe colors are tinted by the background's hue with a
warm/neutral/cool temperature trait (bwSourceColors), gradients are
interpolated in OKLab with 8 sub-stops per segment (addOklabStops) so
they never pass through muddy sRGB midpoints, and the four-corner
source texture is a full per-pixel bilinear blend in OKLab space
(fillFourCornerOklabTexture).


3. FETCHED BLOCK DATA AS DRAWING PARAMETERS
-------------------------------------------

After the deterministic render completes, the piece opens a connection
to Ethereum mainnet: every 12 seconds it calls eth_getBlockByNumber
("latest") against a rotating list of five public RPC endpoints, with a
5-second timeout and automatic failover (the last endpoint that worked
becomes the preferred one). Block numbers are grouped into epochs of 4
blocks (~48 seconds), and only an epoch change mutates state — the
piece breathes on roughly the timescale of a song section, not per
block.

The block hash is treated as a control-voltage source. Individual bytes
are mapped onto drawing parameters:

- Bytes 0–4 become noise domain offsets: the base field's x/y read
  position shifts by up to +/-0.12, the harmonic field's by up to
  +/-0.75 in x/y and +/-1.5 in z. The block literally moves the read
  head over the Perlin field — the loop replays, but the tape has been
  nudged.

- Bytes 5–7 (plus the epoch number) select per-channel bit masks
  (0xf0, 0xe0, 0xc0, ...) used by the "mask" decompose modes, which
  AND the source color channels before optional ordered dithering.
  Each epoch bit-crushes the colors differently — a changing amount of
  quantization grit, like swapping the bit depth on a sampler.

- Bytes 29–30 (plus the epoch) choose which white stripe of the "rs"
  striped source texture is replaced by a full OKLab palette gradient.
  On each new epoch the texture is rebuilt and the gradient band jumps
  to a different stripe, with a guard so it never lands on the same
  stripe twice in a row.

When an epoch changes, noise statistics are re-measured so the DC
centering stays correct for the shifted field, and the live loop keeps
scanning: drawLiveContinuousRows repaints one source row per animation
frame, wrapping the row cursor modulo the image height and resetting
the column-melt accumulator at each wraparound. This is the looping
device made explicit — a playhead endlessly circling the same material,
overdubbing semi-transparent passes (alpha 120–200) whose character
shifts each time the chain hands it a new epoch.

The design degrades gracefully. If every RPC endpoint fails, or before
the first fetch resolves, the same five offsets are derived from bytes
24–28 of the token hash instead (liveStateFromHash), so the piece is
fully deterministic and self-contained offline; the chain is an
enrichment, not a dependency. A visibility check pauses polling in
hidden tabs, and the live state (source, block number, epoch) is
exposed on globalThis.__textureMelt and broadcast via
"runners-standing-still:live-block" CustomEvents for viewers and
tooling.


THE LOOPER, SUMMARIZED
----------------------

Record: the token hash seeds a paired sfc32 PRNG and the hash-bit
traits, and a single deterministic pass renders the phrase — noise
integrated into melt, colored by the OKLab palette.

Loop: once complete, a playhead re-scans the image row by row, forever,
at one row per frame.

Overdub: each pass lays down translucent color over the last, its
accumulator resetting only when the loop wraps.

Tweak the knobs: every four blocks, Ethereum reaches in and shifts the
noise read position, the channel masks, and the gradient stripe — the
performer's hand on the pedal while the loop keeps running.
