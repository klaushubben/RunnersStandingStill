RUNNERS STANDING STILL — READINGS AGAINST THE TRANS AM SONG
============================================================

The title comes from "Runners Standing Still" by Trans Am. The full
lyric (Lyrics.txt) is four short stanzas:

    speaking softly
    toward you
    two rounded words

    if the air
    was transparent
    i'd float away

    sudden songs
    resemble
    runners standing still

    if the air
    was electric
    we'd float away

Below are a few different ways the script and the artwork can be read
against it. They aren't mutually exclusive — they're more like
alternate takes.


READING 1: MOTION THAT PRODUCES STILLNESS
-----------------------------------------

The central image of the song — a runner at full stride who appears
frozen — is the piece's exact mechanical condition. After the initial
render completes, the artwork never stops working: a playhead repaints
one row every animation frame, forever, wrapping around the image like
a runner circling a track. And yet to a viewer the picture is
essentially still. All that running accumulates into an image that
stands.

Trans Am's music does the same thing — motorik repetition, a beat that
drives relentlessly forward without ever seeming to arrive anywhere.
The melt algorithm is that idea drawn: each column is a random walk
that only ever moves, integrated into a composition that only ever
holds. The runner is the row cursor; the standing still is the canvas.


READING 2: TWO ROUNDED WORDS
----------------------------

"Speaking softly / toward you / two rounded words" — the piece is
built almost entirely out of soft pairs.

Two noise voices: a base field and a harmonic field, blended by a mix
knob, never one alone. Two interleaved PRNGs (prngA and prngB),
alternating draw by draw like two voices trading syllables. Two palette
families. Two melt accumulators, one deterministic and one live. And
"rounded" is literal: Perlin noise is the rounded word — its quintic
fade curve exists precisely to remove corners, to make every gradient
transition smooth as breath. A love letter to classic Perlin noise is
a love letter to roundedness itself.

The "speaking softly" is in the alpha. The live loop overdubs at
roughly half opacity — nothing is ever stated loudly or all at once;
the image is arrived at by murmuring the same phrase over itself until
it becomes solid.


READING 3: TRANSPARENT AIR, ELECTRIC AIR
-----------------------------------------

The song offers two conditional atmospheres: transparent air and
electric air. The script has exactly these two modes.

Transparent air is the fallback state: no network, no chain, just the
token hash. The piece runs perfectly well in it — deterministic,
self-contained, sealed like a vacuum. "I'd float away": alone, in
transparent air, the drift offsets come from the token's own hash
bytes; the piece floats on nothing but itself.

Electric air is when the RPC connects and Ethereum starts humming
through the artwork — an invisible ambient charge that permeates the
piece without ever being depicted. Every four blocks the electricity
shifts the noise read head, re-crushes the color channels, moves the
gradient band to a new stripe. "We'd float away": the plural is the
point. In electric air, the piece is no longer alone — it drifts in
step with everyone else watching the same chain, a shared atmosphere
moving all tokens of the collection at once, together.


READING 4: SUDDEN SONGS
-----------------------

"Sudden songs / resemble / runners standing still" — read the epoch
change as the sudden song. Every four blocks, without warning, the
piece receives a new set of instructions: new offsets, new masks, a
gradient that jumps stripes. It's an abrupt event, a song starting
mid-silence.

And yet nothing visibly lurches. The playhead just keeps circling at
one row per frame, and the new epoch's character seeps in gradually as
the loop passes over old material. The sudden song arrives, and the
runner keeps standing still — change is only ever revealed at the speed
of the loop. This is also how a looper pedal behaves: you can stomp a
new setting instantly, but the audience only hears it as the loop comes
back around.


READING 5: THE LOOP AS A LOVE SONG
-----------------------------------

The gentlest reading: the song is someone speaking softly toward
another person, and the script is the artist speaking softly toward an
algorithm. The piece doesn't use Perlin noise as an ingredient; it
addresses it — implements it by hand in the original 1980s idiom,
permutation table and quintic fade and all, then spends the whole
runtime tracing and retracing its contours like a phrase you can't
stop repeating. A loop is what a love song becomes when you refuse to
let it end. The runner stands still because it never wants to leave.
